Why Processing Plants Switch to PP Lugs

Wood and fibreglass lugs have one problem in common: surface degradation. Wood splinters, fibreglass crazes — both create micro-fissures where bacteria colonise and survive sanitisation. PP meat lugs have no such failure mode.

The moulded PP surface is genuinely smooth at the microscopic level. Combined with the tapered draft angle on all four walls, product releases cleanly and cleaning water drains completely — leaving no pooled moisture for biofilm to form overnight.

  • No surface cracks, crazes, or splinters possible
  • Tapered walls — product and water release fully
  • Pressure-wash and chlorine bleach safe
  • No odour absorption between product runs

Choosing the Right Meat Lug for Your Operation

Meat lugs come in a wider range of configurations than most buyers realise. Getting the specification right before ordering saves significant cost and avoids the operational friction of lugs that don't stack correctly or don't fit your conveyor system.

Capacity and Dimensions

The most common meat lug sizes run from 20-litre (used for portion cuts and packaging lines) up to 60-litre (used for primal cuts, bone-in product, and bulk transfer). The standard 30-litre lug is the workhorse of most processing operations — it's the size that fits a standard processing table depth, holds a manageable weight for manual handling (around 25–30 kg loaded), and stacks 8 high empty for storage.

Custom sizes are available from Elipacko for operations with specific conveyor widths, processing table heights, or blast-freezer shelf dimensions. If you're retrofitting lugs into an existing system, bring the shelf or table dimensions to the quoting conversation.

Colour Coding for HACCP Zone Separation

HACCP plans for multi-species processing facilities typically require physical or visual separation of red meat, poultry, and seafood processing zones. Colour-coded lugs are one of the most reliable methods — a blue lug never leaves the fish hall, a red lug never enters the poultry area.

Elipacko compounds colour directly into the PP resin, so the colour is integral to the wall — it doesn't fade, peel, or scrub off in harsh cleaning environments. Standard colour options include white, blue, red, yellow, and green. Custom RAL colours are available at volume.

Lid and Cover Options

For blast-freezer storage and cold-chain transport, lids are essential. A close-fitting lid maintains product temperature, prevents cross-contamination between stacked lugs, and reduces freezer burn on exposed surfaces. Elipacko offers snap-fit and press-fit lid options. Specify whether you need sealed (airtight) or vented lids depending on your blast-freeze cycle and defrost protocol.

Meat Lug FAQs

Can plastic meat lugs go in a blast freezer?

Yes. Elipacko's meat lugs are rated to −30°C continuous use. The PP formulation maintains impact resistance at these temperatures — it won't crack or become brittle from the rapid temperature drop in a blast freezer. Specify your freezer temperature when ordering to confirm the right grade.

Are plastic lugs suitable for raw meat contact?

Yes, when manufactured from NSF-listed PP resin. Elipacko's food-grade lugs meet FDA 21 CFR 177.1520 requirements for direct food contact. Request the material compliance documentation with your order — it will reference the specific resin and the applicable food contact standard.

How do you sanitise plastic meat lugs?

Standard sanitisation protocols for PP meat lugs: rinse with cold water to remove gross contamination, wash with hot water (up to 85°C) and food-grade detergent, sanitise with chlorine solution (up to 200 ppm) or QAC-based sanitiser, final rinse. PP is fully compatible with all standard food processing sanitisers including peroxyacetic acid. Do not use phenolic-based sanitisers — these can leave residues that affect PP surface properties over time.

How many times can a plastic meat lug be reused?

Under normal processing conditions with correct cleaning, Elipacko PP meat lugs are designed for 100+ use cycles before showing significant wear. The main degradation mechanism is UV exposure and repeated impact — both of which are controlled in a typical indoor processing environment.

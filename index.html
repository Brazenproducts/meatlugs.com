What Are Meat Lugs?

Meat lugs — also called meat tubs, processing tubs, or offal bins — are rigid, rectangular plastic containers designed for the meat industry. They serve as the primary handling vessel for raw protein throughout the processing chain: from the kill floor and offal collection, through chilling and aging, right up to packing and dispatch.

Unlike improvised containers such as cardboard boxes or lined crates, a proper meat lug is purpose-engineered. The geometry, wall thickness, and polymer specification are all chosen for wet, chilled, and heavily loaded environments where hygiene and food safety are paramount.

Why PP Is the Right Material

Polypropylene (PP) is the right material for food-contact plastic containers in meat processing. Its closed-cell structure means bacteria cannot colonize the surface. PP does not absorb odors or fats, does not corrode, and withstands the high-pressure hot water and chemical sanitizing cycles that abattoirs run between shifts.

Copolymer PP maintains toughness and impact resistance at blast-freeze temperatures as low as -20°F — making it the correct choice for cold-chain applications from cutting room to frozen storage. Food-grade PP is formulated with only additives approved under FDA 21 CFR 177.1520, the federal standard for direct food-contact plastics in the United States.

PP's smooth, non-porous surface supports HACCP biofilm-prevention requirements. Internal radii are crevice-free, eliminating harboring points that accumulate microbial contamination in poorly designed containers. A PP meat lug sanitized with standard food-contact cleaners — quaternary ammonium compounds, peracetic acid, or chlorinated solutions — is fully restored to hygienic service, trip after trip. PP is also fully recyclable at end of service life under resin code #5, unlike single-use cardboard alternatives.

Stackable and Nestable Design

Stacking full means loaded lugs can be placed directly on top of one another without crushing the contents or destabilizing the stack. Stacking rails molded into the base locate precisely onto the rim of the unit below, preventing lateral shift during forklift movement. Chilling rooms can be used efficiently with pallets stacked four or five lugs high.

Nesting empty means returned, washed lugs take up far less space. A standard lug typically nests to approximately 60% of its loaded stack height — nearly twice as many empty lugs in the same footprint at the receiving bay, washing room, or during return transport.

Color Coding for HACCP Compliance

Many processing plants use a HACCP color-coding system: red lugs for raw red meat, yellow for poultry, white for cooked product, blue for fish. This visual segregation is one of the most straightforward cross-contamination controls available. Food-grade PP pigments are stable under repeated chemical washing — they do not fade to an ambiguous off-color that would undermine your color-coding protocol.

Applications in the Abattoir and Beyond

Offal Collection

Offal lugs are positioned directly under the evisceration line to catch hearts, livers, kidneys, tongues, and intestines as they are removed. Smooth interior surfaces and optional perforated bases allow blood and wash water to drain freely. Handles and lifting points must be robust enough for repeated manual handling by operatives wearing heavy gloves.

Trim and Mincing Operations

In deboning and trim rooms, meat lugs serve as the primary collection vessel for every grade of trim. Accurate batch control starts with dedicated, labeled lugs per grade. High-capacity lugs allow a deboning operative to work longer before a lug needs emptying, improving line flow.

Bone and By-Product Handling

Bone collection requires thicker walls (5–6 mm at the base), reinforced corner junctions, and a base geometry that distributes point loads effectively. Using under-specified containers for bone leads to cracking and eventual failure — creating a foreign-body hazard that no food-safety program can afford.

Cold Chain and Blast Chilling

Lugs used in blast chillers must withstand sudden thermal shock. Copolymer PP retains its impact resistance at -20°F, ensuring lugs do not become brittle or prone to shattering. This cold-chain capability is a non-negotiable requirement for USDA FSIS-regulated facilities and commercial export abattoirs.

Maintenance and Service Life

A meat lug is a capital asset. With proper care, a high-quality PP lug should last five to ten years in commercial processing. Key maintenance steps:

  • Pre-rinse with cold water to remove bulk product before it dries on the surface.
  • Chemical wash with an approved food-contact sanitizer at the concentration specified by your HACCP program.
  • High-pressure rinse to remove all chemical residue.
  • Air-dry or drain upside-down before storage to prevent biofilm formation.
  • Inspect regularly for stress cracks, deep surface scoring, or color changes.
  • Retire promptly any lug showing structural damage — do not extend its life with tape or adhesives.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are meat lugs the same as meat tubs or processing bins?
Yes — "meat lug," "meat tub," "processing tub," and "offal bin" all refer to the same product: a rectangular, food-grade PP container for meat processing. Terminology varies by sector but the product is the same.
Can PP meat lugs be used in a blast freezer?
Yes. Copolymer PP retains its structural integrity and impact resistance down to approximately -20°F, making it fully suitable for blast-chilling and blast-freezing applications.
What sanitizers are compatible with PP meat lugs?
PP is compatible with most food-grade sanitizers including quaternary ammonium compounds (QACs), peracetic acid (PAA), chlorine-based products, and sodium hydroxide solutions at recommended concentrations. Elipacko can provide chemical compatibility data for your specific cleaning program.
How many meat lugs can I stack safely?
Most standard lugs are rated for four to six units high when fully loaded. Exceeding the recommended stack height can cause the base to deflect or stacking locators to disengage, creating a toppling hazard.
Do PP meat lugs comply with FDA food safety regulations?
Yes. Meat lugs manufactured from virgin food-grade copolymer PP comply with FDA 21 CFR 177.1520. Elipacko provides material compliance documentation for qualifying orders to support FSMA, HACCP, and third-party audit programs.
What is the minimum order quantity?
Standard items are typically available in minimum quantities of 50 units. For custom sizes, colors, or embossing, contact Elipacko at elipacko.com.
